Force

A force is a push or pull upon an object resulting from the object's interaction with another object. Whenever there is an interaction between two objects, there is a force upon each of the objects. When the interaction ceases, the two objects no longer experience the force. Forces only exist as a result of an interaction.

Applied Force

An applied force is a force that is applied to an object by a person or another object. If a person is pushing a desk across the room, then there is an applied force acting upon the object. ... On occasions, a normal force is exerted horizontally between two objects that are in contact with each other.

Contact Force

The forces in which two interacting bodies are in physical contact with each other are called contact force.

  • Applied Force.
  • Normal Force.
  • Tensional Force.
  • Muscular Force.
  • Collision Force
  • Frictional Force
  • Mechanical Force

Non Contact Force

The forces which do not require a physical contact between the two interacting bodies act at a distance are called non contact forces.

  • Magnetic Force.
  • Electrostatic Force
  • Gravitational Force.

Normal Force

Normal Force is the support force exerted on a body that is in contact with another stable body. For example if a book is kept on a table then the table exerts an upward force on the book to support its weight.

Illustrations

Tensional Force

In physics, tension is described as the pulling force transmitted axially by the means of a string, a cable, chain, or similar object, or by each end of a rod, truss member, or similar three-dimensional object; tension might also be described as the action-reaction pair of forces acting at each end of said elements.

Illustrations

Muscular Force

It is a force that results because of the action of muscles and is a contact force since there is contact between the surfaces. ... Muscular force is required whenever the movement of the body occurs. Strolling, lifting, getting up from a seat, crossing a leg, and so on all require muscular force.

Illustrations

Collision Force

The contact force which comes into play when two bodies collide with each other is called Colllision Force. For example,when two cars collide,they exert force on each other. This force is collision force.

Illustrations

Frictional Force

The force that opposes the relative motion of two bodies is called friction. It slows down things by acting in the direction opposite to the motion of the moving body. Friction is a contact force as it arises due to contact between the surfaces.

Illustrations

Mechanical Force

A mechanical force can be defined as a force that features some direct contact between two objects (one applying the force and another which is in a state of rest or in a state of motion) and results in the production of a change in the state of the object (state of rest or state of motion).

Illustrations

Magnetic Force

>Magnetic force is a consequence of electromagnetic force and is caused due to the motion of charges. We have learned that moving charges surround itself with a magnetic field. With this context, the magnetic force can be described as a force that arises due to interacting magnetic fields.

Illustrations

ElectroStatic Force

>Electrostatic forces are non-contact forces; they pull or push on objects without touching them. Rubbing some materials together can result in something called 'charge' being moved from one surface to the other. Charged objects pull on other uncharged objects and may either push or pull on other charged objects.

Illustrations

Gravitational Force

Gravity, or gravitation, is a natural phenomenon by which all things with mass or energy—including planets, stars, galaxies, and even light—are attracted to one another. On Earth, gravity gives weight to physical objects, and the Moon's gravity causes the tides of the oceans.
